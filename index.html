Can we solve

Techno-Inequality?

The difference in opportunity between those who have access to technology, and those who do not.

As with any inequality, technological inequality affects all aspects of life. Your ability to find a job, to receive an education, to change your current socioeconomic situation - they are all intrinsically affected by the technologies you have access to.

This is especially true for computer, smartphone & internet access.

A study from 2007 found that "a 10 percentage-point increase in broadband penetration raises annual per-capita growth by 0.9-1.5 percentage points".

There are many variables for why the internet is a driving force for economic development, but arguably one key aspect is that it is a portal for easily accessible software that allows individuals to automate processes as well as connect with individuals they would otherwise not come in contact with.

Approaching The Problem

This project proposes that there are three main causes of techno-inequality:

  1. Access to the internet is limited in parts of the world due to either infrastructure limitations or government policy
  2. Not all individuals can afford a broadband or 3G/4G subscription
  3. Not all individuals can afford the devices required to access the internet.

While the obvious approach would be to tackle one of these directly, these are very large and complex issues.

You could campaign for governments to improve internet access, find a way to subsidise internet access, you could donate devices or develop cheaper ones.

However all of these solutions require time and - more importantly - substantial capital.

In the absence of time and capital, we still have the ability to affect change via smaller, targeted solutions.

While not a direct solution to this problem, this project proposes a small solution with the potential to make a sizable impact - a microintervention.

Instead of focusing on providing the technology itself - why not attempt to provide a more cost-effective solution with the same benefits?

Older cellphones (not smartphones) are much cheaper devices, and since the technology has been around since 1983 cell tower infrastructure is widely available even in relatively poor countries.

According to sources only 50% of the world's population having access to 3G or 4G networks, and a measly 9.8% of the world's population has acess to broadband. In comparison, mobile cellular penetration was almost at 100% in 2013.

Data Sources: Pew Global, World Bank, PCMag, Tech Crunch, Deccan Herald, Wikipedia

Why not use existing cellphone technology and infrastructure to create useful services that interface with the internet?

SMaaS: Short Messaging as a Service

SMaaS is a portmanteau of two terms:

  1. SMS:
    Short Message Service: a system for sending short text messages, as from one cell phone to another or from a computer to a cell phone.
  2. SaaS
    Software as a Service (SaaS) is a software distribution model in which applications are hosted by a vendor or service provider and made available to customers over a network, typically the Internet.

These kind of services already exist in some parts of the world, for example, MTA Bus Time which allows you to check when buses are arriving at a stop.

There's also the much hyped Magic which you basically text to have someone act as your virtual assistant

The Designs

There are two main challenges of creating a SMS-based interface:

  1. Conciseness
    You must keep any interaction that needs to be in the same message under 160 characters
  2. Instructional
    The user needs to be able to understand how to use your service with very little guidance as the only way for them to learn how to use your service is likely to be in the service itself
